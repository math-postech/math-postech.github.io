Lecture 13: Laplace Expansion, Adjugate & Cramer's Rule ​

Topics: §4.4–4.5 — Cofactor (algebraic complement), Laplace expansion (column/row), cofactor orthogonality, adjugate matrix A∗, the equation A∗A=(detA)I, inverse formula A−1=1detAA∗, invertibility criterion (detA≠0), Cramer's rule Date: May 4 – May 7, 2026

Overview ​

Lecture 12 established the three determinant axioms and the cross-filling formula det=a1⋯an⋅det(S). This lecture discovers that multilinearity (P1) lets us expand the determinant along any single column — the Laplace expansion. This leads to the cofactor orthogonality trick, the adjugate matrix, an explicit inverse formula, and Cramer's rule for solving linear systems.

1. From Cross-Filling to Cofactor ​

1.1 The Idea ​

Write one column of A in terms of standard basis vectors:

col j=a1je→1+a2je→2+⋯+anje→n

By multilinearity (P1), the determinant splits into n terms — one for each e→i plugged into column j:

det(A)=∑i=1naij⋅Aij

where the algebraic cofactor Aij is the determinant of the matrix with column j replaced by e→i (all other columns unchanged).

1.2 The 3×3 Example ​

For A=(212131213), expanding along column 1:

det(A)=2⋅A11+1⋅A21+2⋅A31

Each cofactor is computed by plugging e→i into column 1:

  • A11=det(112031013)=8
  • A21=det(012131013)=−1
  • A31=det(012031113)=−5
det(A)=2(8)+1(−1)+2(−5)=16−1−10=5

1.3 Row Expansion ​

Since det(AT)=det(A), we also get expansion along any row:

det(A)=∑j=1naij⋅Aij(along row i)

2. Cofactor Orthogonality ​

2.1 Right Column = det(A) ​

Expanding along column j with column j's own entries gives det(A) — that's just the Laplace expansion.

2.2 Wrong Column = 0 ​

What if we pair column k's entries with column j's cofactors (k≠j)?

∑i=1naik⋅Aij=?

This equals the determinant of a matrix where column j is replaced by column k — but then two columns are identical! By the alternating property (P2): det=0.

2.3 The Orthogonality Formula ​

∑i=1naik⋅Aij={det(A)if k=j0if k≠j

This is the engine behind the adjugate matrix.

3. Adjugate Matrix ​

3.1 2×2 Case First ​

For A=(2113) with det(A)=5:

The adjugate A∗ packs all cofactors (transposed):

A∗=(A11A21A12A22)=(3−1−12)

Shortcut for 2×2: swap diagonal entries, negate off-diagonal entries.

3.2 Why A∗A=det(A)⋅I ​

Multiply A∗⋅A. Each entry of the product is a dot product: row i of A∗ (= cofactors of column i) dotted with column j of A (= entries of column j).

Diagonal entries (i=j): cofactors matched with their own column's entries. This is exactly the Laplace expansion — it equals det(A).

Entry (1,1):2⋅det(1103)⏟A11+1⋅det(0113)⏟A21=det(2113)=5

Off-diagonal entries (i≠j): cofactors of column i matched with column j's entries. The entries go into the wrong column position, creating two identical columns. By P2: det=0.

Entry (1,2):1⋅det(1103)⏟A11+3⋅det(0113)⏟A21=det(1133)=0

3.3 Definition and Theorem ​

Proposition

Definition (Adjugate)

The adjugate (or classical adjoint) of A is:

A∗:=(cofactor matrix)T,(A∗)ij=Aji

Proposition

Theorem (Adjugate Identity)

A∗A=(detA)⋅In

3.4 The Cross-Out Rule ​

For efficient computation of cofactors:

Aij=(−1)i+j⋅det(delete row i,col j from A)

The sign follows a checkerboard pattern: (+−+−+−+−+)

4. Inverse via Adjugate ​

4.1 The Inverse Formula ​

From A∗A=(detA)I, divide by detA:

Proposition

Theorem (Inverse Formula)

If det(A)≠0:

A−1=1det(A)A∗

For our example: A−1=15(8−1−5−120−505)

4.2 2×2 Inverse ​

(abcd)−1=1ad−bc(d−b−ca)

4.3 Invertibility Criterion ​

Proposition

Theorem (Invertibility ⇔ Nonzero Determinant)

A is invertible⟺det(A)≠0

Proof (⇐): If det(A)≠0, we construct A−1=1detAA∗.

Proof (⇒): If A is invertible, then det(A)det(A−1)=det(I)=1, so det(A)≠0.

Contrapositive: det(A)=0 implies A is not invertible (columns are linearly dependent).

5. Cramer's Rule ​

5.1 Setup ​

Given Ax=b with A invertible, we want to find xi.

From the adjugate inverse: x=A−1b=1detAA∗b.

5.2 The Formula ​

The i-th component:

xi=1detA∑k(A∗)ikbk=1detA∑kAki⋅bk

The numerator ∑kAkibk is the Laplace expansion along column i — but with column i's entries replaced by b:

Proposition

Theorem (Cramer's Rule)

xi=det(Ai)det(A)

where Ai is A with column i replaced by b.

5.3 2×2 Example ​

(2113)(xy)=(55)x=det(5153)det(2113)=15−55=105=2y=det(2515)5=10−55=55=1

5.4 3×3 Practice ​

(212131213)(x1x2x3)=(768)

Answer: (x1,x2,x3)=(2,1,1).

Summary ​

All results in this lecture flow from just two axioms:

ResultSource
Laplace expansionP1 (multilinearity) — split one column into e→i pieces
Cofactor orthogonalityP1 + P2 — wrong column creates two identical columns
Adjugate identity A∗A=(detA)IOrthogonality packaged as matrix multiplication
Inverse formulaDivide by detA
Cramer's ruleInverse formula + Laplace expansion of numerator
