Lecture 7: Linear Transformation and Projection ​

Topics: §4.1, §5.1 — Linear Transformations, Projection Operators (P²=P), Sunlight-Floor Model, Column Space and Null Space of Projections Date: Mar 30 – Apr 2, 2026

1. Linear Transformations ​

In this lecture, we shift from viewing matrices as static objects (Lectures 1–6) to viewing them as dynamic transformations that act on vectors.

1.1 Definition ​

Definition

Linear Transformation

A function T:V→W between vector spaces is called a linear transformation if:

T(λv+w)=λT(v)+T(w)

for all v,w∈V and all scalars λ.

Remark

Equivalent Properties

The single condition above is equivalent to the following two properties together:

  1. Additivity: T(v+w)=T(v)+T(w)
  2. Homogeneity: T(λv)=λT(v)

1.2 Geometric Interpretation ​

Geometrically, linear transformations preserve the linear structure of space:

  • Straight lines remain straight lines
  • Parallel lines remain parallel
  • The origin stays fixed: T(0)=0

Example

Example 1.1: Common Linear Transformations in R2

  1. Rotation by 90° counterclockwise: T(xy)=(−yx)

  2. Reflection across x-axis: T(xy)=(x−y)

  3. Scaling by factor 2: T(xy)=(2x2y)

  4. Projection onto x-axis: T(xy)=(x0)

1.3 Matrix Representation ​

Proposition

Matrix Representation Theorem

Every linear transformation T:Rn→Rm can be represented by left multiplication by a matrix:

T(v)=Av

for some m×n matrix A.

Conversely, every matrix defines a linear transformation by v↦Av.

Definition

Linear Operator

When the domain and codomain are the same (T:V→V), we call T a linear operator.

For the rest of this lecture, we focus on linear operators represented by square matrices.

2. Projection Operators ​

Projection operators are the most simple and intuitive, yet also the most important linear transformations.

2.1 Definition ​

Definition

Projection Operator (Idempotent)

A matrix P is called a projection operator (or idempotent) if:

P2=P

The name "idempotent" comes from Latin: idem (same) + potent (power) — applying the operation multiple times gives the same result as applying it once.

2.2 Geometric Explanation: The Sunlight Model ​

We introduce the idea of projection operators through the sunlight model before explaining the definition P2=P.

Example

Example 2.1: Shadows on the Floor

Imagine:

  • A vector v standing straight at the origin (like a tree)
  • Sunlight shining from some direction
  • The projection P maps any vector v to its shadow w:=Pv on the floor

Key observation: The direction of sunlight need not be perpendicular to the floor. In our model, we choose:

  • One direction for the floor
  • One direction for the sunlight

These two directions are independent.

Remark

Why P2=P?

In the definition, we required P2=P. Algebraically, this means P2v=Pv for any vector v.

Geometrically, it means: the shadow of a shadow is the shadow itself.

Why? Since w:=Pv represents the shadow of v, the vector w lies on the floor. The shadow of a vector already on the floor is itself. Therefore Pw=w.

This gives us P(Pv)=Pv, which is exactly P2=P.

2.3 Understanding the Sunlight Model: Three Questions ​

Intuitively, a projection needs to specify the floor and the sunlight direction. But we defined projection only by P2=P. Therefore, we need to answer:

::: question Three Key Questions

  1. If P2=P, how do we define the floor?
  2. If P2=P, how do we define the direction of sunlight?
  3. After specifying these, why is Pv exactly the shadow of v? :::

To answer these questions, we examine what happens to specific types of vectors under an actual projection.

Question 1: What if a vector points toward the sun? ​

Problem

Problem 2.1: Vectors Pointing Toward the Sun

Assume P is an actual projection. If a vector v is pointing toward the sun, what does its shadow look like?

What is w=Pv in this case?

Answer: Pv=0 (no shadow, because the vector points directly toward the light source)

Should v be an element of the following set?

{v∈V:Pv=0}

Yes! This set describes the direction of sunlight.

Question 2: What if a vector lies on the ground? ​

Problem

Problem 2.2: Vectors on the Floor

Assume P is an actual projection. If a vector w is lying on the ground, what does its shadow look like?

What is Pw in this case? Is w the same as Pw?

Answer: Pw=w (a vector on the floor is its own shadow)

Should w be an element of the following set?

{w:w=Pv for some v∈V}

Yes! Furthermore, when P is a projection, the following two statements are equivalent:

w=Pv for some v∈V⟺Pw=w

Which of the following sets describes the floor? (Both do!)

{w:w=Pv for some v∈V}={w∈V:Pw=w}

2.4 Column Space and Null Space ​

From Lectures 4–5, we already know:

Definition

Column Space and Null Space (Review)

For any matrix P:

Null(P):={v:Pv=0}Col(P):={w:w=Pv for some v∈V}

From our geometric analysis above:

  • Null(P) = direction of sunlight
  • Col(P) = the floor

2.5 Why Pv Is the Shadow: The Proof ​

Now we prove that if P2=P, then Pv is indeed the shadow of v when projecting onto Col(P) along the direction parallel to Null(P).

Proposition

Proposition 2.1: P2=P Implies Geometric Projection

Let P be an operator with P2=P. Given v∈V, let w be the geometric shadow of v found by:

  • Drawing the floor Col(P)
  • Drawing the sunlight direction Null(P)
  • Finding where the line through v parallel to Null(P) intersects Col(P)

Then w=Pv.

Proof:

Intuitively, w is the shadow if and only if it satisfies:

  1. Shadows lie on the floor: w∈Col(P)
  2. The line from v to w follows the sunlight direction: v−w∈Null(P)

We must show:

{P2=Pw∈Col(P)v−w∈Null(P)⟹w=Pv

Since w∈Col(P), we have w=Px for some x∈V.

Then:

Pw=P(Px)=P2x=Px=w

Since v−w∈Null(P):

P(v−w)=0Pv−Pw=0Pv=Pw

Combining with Pw=w:

Pv=w

This proves that P2=P implies Pv is the shadow of v when projecting to Col(P) along Null(P). ∎

::: success Summary: Projection Operators

As long as an operator P:V→V satisfies P2=P, it is a projection such that:

  1. For any vector v∈V:

    • Pv represents the shadow
    • v−Pv=(I−P)v is a vector pointing in the direction of the sun

  2. Null(P) describes the direction of sunlight (vectors v with Pv=0)

  3. Col(P) describes the floor (all possible shadows w=Pv)

    • In particular, for any w∈Col(P), we have Pw=w :::

3. Basic Properties of Projections ​

3.1 Column Space Characterization ​

Proposition

Proposition 3.1: Column Space Membership Test

Let P be a projection matrix. For any vector y∈Rn:

y∈Col(P)⟺Py=y

Proof:

(⟹): If y∈Col(P), then y=Px for some x.

Then:

Py=P(Px)=P2x=Px=y

(⟸): If Py=y, then y∈Col(P) by definition of column space. ∎

Remark

Geometric Meaning

This says: a vector is on the floor if and only if it equals its own shadow.

3.2 The Companion Projection: I−P ​

Proposition

Proposition 3.2: I−P Is Also a Projection

If P is a projection (P2=P), then I−P is also a projection.

Proof:

(I−P)2=(I−P)(I−P)=I−P−P+P2=I−2P+P=I−P

Therefore (I−P)2=I−P. ∎

Remark

Geometric Interpretation

If P projects onto the floor, then I−P measures the vertical displacement:

Pv=shadow on the floor(I−P)v=v−Pv=vector from shadow to original position

In the sunlight model:

  • P projects onto the floor
  • I−P projects in the direction of sunlight

3.3 The Interchanging Property ​

Proposition

Proposition 3.3: Column Space and Null Space Interchange

For any projection P:

Col(P)=Null(I−P)Col(I−P)=Null(P)

Proof (first equality):

(⊆): Let v∈Col(P).

Then Pv=v (by Proposition 3.1).

So (I−P)v=v−Pv=0.

Thus v∈Null(I−P).

(⊇): Let v∈Null(I−P).

Then (I−P)v=0, which means v=Pv.

So v∈Col(P) (by Proposition 3.1).

The proof of the second equality is similar. ∎

Remark

Beautiful Phenomenon

The projections P and I−P interchange their column spaces and null spaces!

For PFor I−P
Col(P)=Null(I−P)
Null(P)=Col(I−P)

3.4 Disjoint Property ​

Proposition

Proposition 3.4: Column Space and Null Space Are Disjoint

For any projection P:

Col(P)∩Null(P)={0}

Proof:

Let v∈Col(P)∩Null(P).

Since v∈Col(P):

Pv=v(by Proposition 3.1)

Since v∈Null(P):

Pv=0(by definition of null space)

Therefore v=0. ∎

Remark

Geometric Interpretation

In the sunlight-floor model:

  • Col(P) = the floor
  • Null(P) = the direction of sunlight

These two subspaces only meet at the origin.

A non-zero vector cannot simultaneously be on the floor and point toward the sun.

4. Summary ​

::: success Key Concepts from This Lecture

  1. Linear Transformation: T(λv+w)=λT(v)+T(w)

    • Represented by matrices: T(v)=Av
    • Preserves linear structure

  2. Projection Operator: P2=P

    • Geometric model: sunlight casting shadows on floor
    • Col(P) = the floor (where shadows land)
    • Null(P) = direction of sunlight

  3. I−P is also a projection

    • Measures displacement from floor

  4. Column Space Characterization: y∈Col(P)⟺Py=y

  5. Interchanging Property: Col(P)=Null(I−P) and vice versa

  6. Disjoint Property: Col(P)∩Null(P)={0} :::
